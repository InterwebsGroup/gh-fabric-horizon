## What Is a Wearable Blanket?

Let's start with the basics.

A wearable blanket is exactly what it sounds like — a blanket you can wear. Brands like the Oodie and The Comfy popularized the category. They're made with thick sherpa fleece, often lined with flannel, and they're designed to keep you as warm as possible while you're lounging at home.

Here's what defines a wearable blanket:

- **Thick, heavy fabric** — usually sherpa or plush fleece, sometimes with a flannel outer layer
- **Blanket-like proportions** — extra wide, extra long, puffy all over
- **Indoor-only design** — they're bulky and clearly look like loungewear
- **Maximum warmth** — built for cold rooms and couch sessions
- **Oversized fit** — not fitted in any way, more like draping a blanket over yourself

Wearable blankets are great at what they do. If you want to feel like you're wrapped in a comforter while watching TV, they deliver. Nobody's arguing that.

But they're not hoodies. And that's where the confusion starts.

---

## What Is an Oversized Hoodie?

An oversized hoodie is a hoodie — just bigger. It has the same classic silhouette you already know: hood, kangaroo pocket, ribbed cuffs and hem, standard proportions. It's just sized up for that relaxed, roomy, "borrowed from someone twice your size" fit.

Here's what defines an oversized hoodie:

- **Lighter fabric** — typically fleece or French terry, soft but not heavy
- **Classic hoodie shape** — recognizable silhouette, not blanket-shaped
- **Wearable anywhere** — looks like a normal piece of clothing in public
- **Year-round comfort** — cozy without overheating
- **Styled like a hoodie** — pairs with jeans, leggings, joggers, sneakers

That's [what we make at Giant Hoodies](/blogs/the-hoodie-blog/what-is-a-giant-hoodie). Our hoodies use 8oz sponge fleece — it's soft, plush, and lightweight. Customers describe it as "wearing a hug." It's cozy enough for the couch but normal enough for the coffee shop, the airport, the dog park, or a Sunday grocery run.

The key difference? You can wear an oversized hoodie out your front door without feeling like you forgot to get dressed.

## Why People Confuse Them

It's not your fault. The internet (and a lot of brands) use "blanket hoodie," "wearable blanket hoodie," and "oversized hoodie" interchangeably. Search for any of those terms and you'll get a mix of both product types in the results.

That confusion is actually why we wrote this post. If you type "blanket hoodie" into Google, you deserve to know that there are two completely different products fighting for your attention — and they'll give you very different experiences.

Here's a quick test: if the product looks like something you'd only wear on your couch, it's a wearable blanket. If it looks like something you'd throw on to grab coffee, it's an oversized hoodie.

## Fabric and Feel: The Biggest Difference

This is the core of everything. The fabric changes the weight, the warmth, the look, and where you can wear it.

**Wearable blankets** use thick sherpa fleece — the same fuzzy material you'd find lining a winter jacket or a cozy throw blanket. It's warm. Really warm. Some versions add a flannel or microfiber outer layer, making them even thicker. The result is a product that weighs 2-3 pounds and generates a lot of heat. Great for December. Not so great for April.

**Oversized hoodies** (like Giant Hoodies) use a lighter fleece. Our 8oz sponge fleece is soft and plush — think of your favorite broken-in sweatshirt, but elevated. It's warm enough to be cozy, but breathable enough that you won't overheat. You can wear it in the fall, winter, spring, or on a cool summer evening. That versatility is the whole point.

The feel is different, too. Sherpa is fuzzy and nubby — like petting a stuffed animal. Sponge fleece is smooth, plush, and consistent — the kind of soft that makes you not want to take it off. Both are comfortable, but for all-day wear, most people find the lighter fleece wins.

## Style and Versatility: Where It Really Matters

Let's be real — this is the deciding factor for most people.

A wearable blanket is designed for one setting: home. The thick material, the puffy shape, the blanket-like proportions — it's all built for lounging. And that's fine. If you're buying it for movie nights and work-from-home days, it does the job. But you're not wearing it to Target. You're not wearing it to pick up your kids. You're not wearing it on a plane. It's a single-purpose product.

An oversized hoodie works everywhere. The silhouette is familiar — everyone recognizes a hoodie. It [pairs with jeans, leggings, shorts, joggers](/blogs/the-hoodie-blog/how-to-style-oversized-hoodie), whatever. You can wear it to brunch, to the gym, to the airport, around the house, to a bonfire, to class. Nobody looks twice. Actually, that's not true — people do look, but it's to ask where you got it.

## Warmth vs. Year-Round Comfort

If maximum warmth is your only goal, a wearable blanket wins. Full stop. They're built for it. Thick sherpa + flannel + oversized fit = cocoon-level warmth. If you live in a cold climate and your house is drafty, a wearable blanket will keep you toasty.

But for most people, "maximum warmth" isn't actually what they want. They want to be *comfortable*. And there's a difference.

Comfortable means cozy without sweating. It means being able to wear the same thing on the couch in October and on an evening walk in May. It means not having to change out of your loungewear before answering the door.

That's where an oversized hoodie shines. The lighter fabric regulates temperature better. You stay warm when it's cool and don't overheat when it's mild. It's the year-round comfort piece that a wearable blanket can't be.
